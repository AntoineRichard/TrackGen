\section{Generation Methods}
\label{sec:generation-methods}

Course generation is best understood as a transformation of a
task-defining spatial object, rather than as a property of a simulator, file
format, or benchmark.  A pipeline can construct a new road network, modify a
gate sequence, reject an infeasible candidate, retain a previously generated
course for later replay, and emit an exchange artifact.  These are different
operations, and several may occur in one implementation.  Following the
boundaries in \cref{sec:scope-definitions}, this section separates
course-changing mechanisms from management of an existing artifact population.
The resulting organization is deliberately orthogonal: it describes what
changes course structure, what operation is applied to a candidate or
population, and how validity is enforced.  It is an analytic classification, not
a ranking.

\subsection{Mechanisms That Change Course Structure}

\paragraph{Constructive and rule-based generation.}
Constructive methods map explicit parameters, primitives, grammars, or
assembly rules to course structure.  The principal advantage is an inspectable
connection from parameters to geometry and topology.  For example, PGDrive
represents road blocks with sockets and parameters such as lane count, width,
and curvature, then docks compatible blocks while building a road network
\cite{Li2020ImprovingGeneralization}.  JunctionArt likewise constructs
high-definition road networks from control lines and intersection rules,
including lane-connectivity geometry that can be exported to OpenDRIVE
\cite{Muktadir2023ProceduralGeneration}.  Such systems can expose useful
semantic controls, including intersection degree, corridor width, or route
structure, but their reachable design space is bounded by the primitive set and
the validity assumptions embedded in its composition rules.  Human-authored
control lines or template choices can be part of a constructive workflow; that
authoring role does not turn a fixed authored course into a generator.

\paragraph{Stochastic procedural generation.}
Stochasticity is a separate mechanism, not a synonym for constructive
generation.  It is present when random choices determine alternative spatial
structure, such as a selected road-block type, a sampled curvature parameter,
or a randomized gate layout.  In the PGDrive construction, random block and
parameter selection is coupled to a deterministic socket-assembly rule
\cite{Li2020ImprovingGeneralization}.  This combination is useful for
producing a training distribution with broad support while retaining a clear
parameterization.  Its costs are uneven coverage and potentially high rejection
or backtracking cost near difficult constraints.  A seed records a particular
draw; it does not by itself establish either novelty or feasibility.

\paragraph{Search and evolutionary generation.}
Search-based methods iteratively retain, vary, or return candidates according
to an objective, fitness, or comparison rule.  They are especially appropriate
when the desired property is relational or performance-conditioned: a test case
may be valuable because it exposes a failure mode, or a course may be selected
because it has a target curvature, visibility, or difficulty profile.  In an
early racing example, a fixed-length sequence of straight or curved segments
with width choices is mutated and retained under entertainment-oriented fitness
criteria \cite{Togelius2006MakingRacing}.  Search-based road testing similarly
uses candidate geometry to pursue target behaviors rather than merely sampling
plausible layouts \cite{Blattner2024DiversityGuided}.  The gain is objective
directed exploration of sparse or adversarial regions; the tradeoff is that the
returned distribution depends on representation, objective, simulator,
budget, and selection pressure.  A high fitness score is not a general validity
certificate, and a search objective should not be confused with the course
traversal semantics themselves.

\paragraph{Learned generative generation.}
Learned generative methods use a trained model to emit course state,
parameters, or geometry.  Hierarchical learned map generation, for example,
models high-definition-map structure as a graph-generative problem
\cite{Mi2021HDMapGenHierarchical}; learned driving-environment models similarly
aim to synthesize scene structure rather than choose from a fixed set of maps
\cite{Rowe2025ScenarioDreamer}.  This axis can capture regularities not easily
encoded as hand-written rules and can condition generation on a desired context.
It also introduces a fidelity problem: generated structure must satisfy the
course contract and any target distributional claim must be evaluated rather
than inferred from visual plausibility.  Training data provenance, conditioning
variables, deterministic reconstruction, and the cost of validation or repair
therefore belong in the report described in \cref{sec:metrics}.

\paragraph{Feedback-driven environment design.}
Feedback-driven environment design adapts a distribution of courses using
learner, agent, or task-performance information.  It is distinct from a learned
generator because feedback can guide a procedural teacher, a search procedure,
or selection from an existing population.  Replay-guided adversarial
environment design makes the distinction explicit: a teacher can propose new
levels while a curator prioritizes previously encountered levels for replay,
with the two roles responding to learner feedback \cite{Jiang2021ReplayGuided}.
Open-ended coevolution similarly treats environment and solution populations as
coupled objects \cite{Wang2019POETOpen}.  The benefit is an adaptive curriculum
that can target the changing competence boundary of a policy.  The principal
risk is a moving and policy-dependent training distribution: performance may
reflect the curator, generator, reward, and feedback schedule as much as the
spatial course mechanism.  Evaluation therefore requires a separately frozen or
reproducibly sampled suite.

\subsection{Operations on Courses and Populations}

Mechanism axes describe how spatial alternatives arise.  A second axis describes
the evidenced operation performed on a particular candidate or population.
\emph{Synthesis} emits new course-defining structure.  \emph{Whole-course
mutation} transforms an already complete candidate, for example by replacing
segments or moving control points; the evolutionary track representation above
has this explicit role \cite{Togelius2006MakingRacing}.  \emph{Selection and
replay} choose among complete extant courses, perhaps using learning potential
or regret.  They can be indispensable for curriculum design, but they do not
create new spatial structure when the underlying courses remain unchanged
\cite{Jiang2021ReplayGuided}.  \emph{Repair and projection} alter a proposed
course to satisfy a declared condition; a chain-code track generator illustrates
a workflow in which a candidate construction is followed by a repair-oriented
stage \cite{doNascimento2021ProceduralGeneration}.

Serialization and interchange are adjacent artifact-management operations, not
generation.  An OpenDRIVE export, for example, may preserve or translate road
geometry and allow other tools to instantiate meshes, waypoints, or traffic, yet
it does not by itself choose a new route or corridor \cite{Muktadir2023ProceduralGeneration}.
Similarly, CommonRoad and OpenSCENARIO establish reusable scenario or route
interfaces without constituting course constructors
\cite{Althoff2017CommonRoadComposable,ASAM2024ASAMOpenSCENARIO}.  This
separation matters in hybrid pipelines.  A constructive sampler with rejection,
an evolutionary mutator followed by projection, or a feedback-driven generator
with replay contains several operations, but it should be described as a staged
pipeline rather than multiplied into several independent method observations.

\subsection{Validity as an Independent Axis}

Validity answers a different question: whether a generated course, its
representation, or its realization satisfies an explicit predicate.  It can be
enforced by construction, by rejection, by an objective penalty, by repair or
projection, by a constraint solver, or by simulation-based validation.  PGDrive,
for instance, tests whether a proposed block intersects the assembled network
and backtracks when it cannot continue \cite{Li2020ImprovingGeneralization};
JunctionArt reports an intersection validator that can discard unrealistically
sharp constructions \cite{Muktadir2023ProceduralGeneration}.  These mechanisms
are evidence about the relationship between a candidate and a predicate, not a
separate generation family.

The predicate itself must name the abstraction level.  Topological closure,
non-self-intersection, boundary clearance, curvature limits, ordered gate
crossing, and successful simulator import are all useful checks, but none implies
the others.  In particular, an imported artifact can be syntactically valid while
remaining dynamically infeasible for a declared vehicle envelope.  Repair must
retain lineage to the raw candidate, and reports should distinguish raw output,
rejected output, repaired output, and simulator conformance as specified in
\cref{sec:metrics,sec:benchmark-protocol}.

\subsection{Purpose Conditions the Pipeline}

The same spatial mechanism has different obligations when it supports training,
testing, or benchmark construction.  For \emph{training}, generation defines a
sampling distribution and may legitimately adapt through feedback or replay;
the curriculum, its seed contract, and its interaction with policy updates are
part of the method.  Procedural environments have been used precisely to vary
training levels and study generalization beyond a fixed set
\cite{Cobbe2020LeveragingProcedural,Li2020ImprovingGeneralization}.  For
\emph{testing}, the aim may be to expose a boundary case, cover a declared
descriptor space, or search for a violation.  Search objectives and any
oracle-dependent simulation outcomes must then be reported with their budget and
failure taxonomy, rather than presented as generic course quality.

For \emph{benchmark construction}, generation is upstream of curation.  A
benchmark requires an immutable or deterministically reconstructible suite,
course identifiers, a representation and realization contract, and an explicit
separation from the data used to fit policies or tune generators.  Selection can
make a suite balanced or diverse, but a fixed selected suite is an evaluation
artifact, not a continuing generator.  Across all three purposes, the central
reporting unit is therefore the declared pipeline and population: mechanism
axes, operations, validity predicates, and artifact-management stages should be
stated together without inferring a method frequency from co-occurring labels.
