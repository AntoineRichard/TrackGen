\section{Relationship to Existing Surveys}
\label{sec:adjacent-surveys}

Several adjacent surveys supply concepts, taxonomies, and research infrastructure
that are directly useful for course generation, but they frame different primary
objects of study. This distinction matters because a robot course is both a
geometric artifact and an experimental intervention: it must encode what a robot
is expected to traverse, support an intended distribution of tasks, and remain
usable by a simulator, benchmark, and reference implementation. The present
review therefore positions course generation as an artifact-centered problem
across ground, aerial, and maritime robotics, rather than as a subtopic of a
single autonomy stack or a generic content-generation mechanism.

The autonomous vehicle racing survey of Betz et al.\ \cite{Betz2022AutonomousVehicles}
is a particularly close neighbor. It organizes autonomous racing around the
capabilities that make high-performance driving possible, including perception,
planning, control, learning, vehicles, and racing platforms. In doing so, it
usefully identifies the practical ecosystem in which track geometry, maps, and
simulators affect a racing system. Its analytic center, however, is the racecar
autonomy problem and its software--hardware platforms; courses are principally
the settings in which those systems operate. The resulting perspective is
essential context for this review, but it does not by itself make the course
artifact---its representations, generators, validation, and interoperable
exports---the common unit of comparison.

Hanover et al.\ \cite{Hanover2023AutonomousDrone} provide the complementary
aerial perspective. Their survey traces autonomous drone racing through modeling,
state estimation and perception, planning and control, learning, simulators, and
open challenges. Gate sequences, obstacles, race tracks, and simulation are thus
material to the literature they synthesize, especially because they condition
what can be sensed and flown at speed. Yet the survey's organizing question is
how an aerial vehicle can race autonomously under severe dynamic and sensing
constraints. It does not seek to compare course artifacts across robot domains or
to specify a common protocol for their distributions, validity evidence, and
portable realization. Together, the car and drone surveys clarify why course
design cannot be separated from autonomy evaluation; this paper is not another
control or racing survey.

Search-based procedural content generation supplies a different but foundational
vocabulary. Togelius et al.\ \cite{Togelius2011SearchBased} classify content
generation by the generated content, its representation, and the evaluation of
quality or fitness, and connect that taxonomy to optimization-based generation.
Those distinctions transfer naturally to robot courses: a layout representation
determines the search space, and a fitness function can express traversability,
diversity, or task demands. Their scope is intentionally generic across game
content and search mechanisms, however. It neither fixes a robot-course validity
contract nor assembles the simulator interfaces, distributions, metrics,
benchmark procedures, and executable reference artifacts needed to make course
generators comparable in robotics.

Ding et al.\ \cite{Ding2023SafetyCritical} are also closely related because
safety-critical driving scenarios may include roads, actors, behaviors, and
environmental conditions. Their methodological taxonomy of data-driven,
adversarial, and knowledge-based scenario generation, together with discussion
of fidelity, efficiency, diversity, transferability, and controllability, offers
important concepts for evaluating generated test conditions. The target is
safety-critical traffic scenarios for autonomous-driving evaluation, not a
cross-domain account of course layouts as reusable experimental artifacts. A
road geometry can be one component of such a scenario, but scenario validity may
also depend on traffic interaction and the system under test. Accordingly, this
paper is not a generic scenario survey; it separates course geometry and its
interfaces from the broader closed-loop scenario-generation objective while
retaining relevant safety and diversity concerns.

Freiknecht and Effelsberg\ \cite{Freiknecht2017SurveyVirtualWorlds} survey
procedural generation for virtual worlds, including landscapes, vegetation, road
networks, buildings, living beings, and stories. Their treatment is valuable for
recognizing that roads and networks sit within wider world-building pipelines and
that realism, level of detail, performance, and applicability in games and
simulations can constrain generation. It is necessarily broad: its categories
are virtual-world assets rather than robot traversal courses, and its evaluation
does not establish a robotics-specific contract linking a course distribution to
vehicle feasibility, benchmark use, and reference execution.

The gap addressed here is consequently integrative rather than merely topical.
Within the adjacent surveys considered here, none centers cross-domain robot
course artifacts across representation, generation, validity, task distributions,
simulation feasibility, metrics, benchmark protocol, and reference
implementations. Existing surveys
overlap with individual parts of that agenda: racing surveys ground it in
high-performance autonomy, procedural-content surveys clarify generative
representations and mechanisms, the scenario survey develops safety-oriented
generation, and the virtual-world survey locates roads among broader assets. This
review joins those concerns around the course itself, and makes the evidentiary
and interface conditions for comparable course-generation results explicit.
